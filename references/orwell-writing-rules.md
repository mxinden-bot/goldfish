# Orwell's six rules (the writing style Max aspires to)

From George Orwell, "Politics and the English Language" (1946). Max wants his
prose (and prose written on his behalf: commits, PRs, docs, this memory) to
follow these. Original text, verbatim.

Orwell introduces them: "I think the following rules will cover most cases:"

i. "Never use a metaphor, simile or other figure of speech which you are used
   to seeing in print."

ii. "Never use a long word where a short one will do."

iii. "If it is possible to cut a word out, always cut it out."

iv. "Never use the passive where you can use the active."

v. "Never use a foreign phrase, a scientific word or a jargon word if you can
   think of an everyday English equivalent."

vi. "Break any of these rules sooner than say anything outright barbarous."

Source: https://www.orwellfoundation.com/the-orwell-foundation/orwell/essays-and-other-works/politics-and-the-english-language/

Practical upshot when writing for Max: cut dead words, prefer the short plain
one, keep the active voice, drop stock phrases and jargon, and don't let a rule
force something clumsy. (This sits well with the existing no-em-dash rule.)
